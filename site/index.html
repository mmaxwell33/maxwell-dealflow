Common questions

The stuff people ask before they call.

If your question isn't here, just email me — happy to talk through anything.

Do you charge for an initial consultation?

No. The first conversation — in person, on the phone, or over video — is always free. Even if you're 12 months away from being ready to buy or sell, that conversation costs you nothing and helps both of us know whether we're a good fit.

How much do I need saved before I start looking?

As an estimate: for a typical Newfoundland home around $350K, you'd want a minimum 5% down payment — roughly $17,500 in that example — plus closing costs of around 1.5–2% of the purchase price. These are rough working numbers to plan around.

Below 20% down, you'll also pay CMHC insurance, which gets rolled into your mortgage. Your lender will run your exact numbers when you go for pre-approval — these vary by income, credit, and the specific property.

What does "pre-approval" actually mean — and do I need it?

A pre-approval is a lender's written commitment to lend you up to a specific amount, based on your income, credit, and debts. It's not a guarantee — the lender still has to approve the specific property — but it tells you (and any seller) what you can credibly offer.

Yes, I'd want you pre-approved before we tour anything serious. It keeps us from falling in love with a house we can't actually finance.

How long does it typically take from accepted offer to closing?

In Newfoundland, 30–60 days is the normal range, depending on financing, inspection, and lawyer availability. Faster than that is possible if everyone's on the ball; slower happens occasionally when something complex turns up in the inspection or title search.

I'll give you a realistic timeline at the offer stage, not a salesy one.

How do you decide what to list my home for?

A Comparable Market Analysis (CMA). I pull every recent sale within a reasonable radius and adjust for condition, lot, finishes, and current market direction. Then I give you an honest range — usually a low/likely/high — and explain the tradeoffs at each price point.

If your number and my number disagree by a lot, I'll tell you why. I won't list a property I think is overpriced just to win the listing.

Who pays the real estate commission when I'm selling?

In Newfoundland the standard convention is that the seller's proceeds cover both the listing agent's and the buyer's agent's commission. I'll show you the full breakdown before you sign anything — no surprises at closing.

Do I really need a home inspection?

Almost always, yes. A solid inspection typically runs around $500–$800 — speak to a couple of inspectors for their exact quote — and surfaces issues a buyer would otherwise inherit: foundation, roof, electrical, plumbing, suspicious moisture. On older Newfoundland housing stock, inspections regularly turn up several thousand dollars of "good to know" repairs you'd otherwise be blind to.

I work with a small list of inspectors I trust to give straight answers, not soft pedal.

What if I don't end up buying or selling?

Then you don't, and we part as friends. No fees, no guilt, no follow-up sales pressure. About a third of the people who reach out aren't ready yet — and most of them come back when they are.
